# Palau

## Status

- Thermal stress occurred in 2024 but coral thermal tolerance has been increasing at 0.1C/decade (published science).
- **Warning sign:** Jellyfish Lake golden jellyfish collapsed from 5 million to fewer than 5,600 (see below — recovering as of 2026).
- Blue Corner, Blue Hole, wall diving = defined by fish density and sharks, largely independent of coral cover. Remain world-class.
- Hard coral diversity is high (~400 hard coral species, ~300 soft coral species, ~1,400 reef fish species). A 2024 report noted some bleached table corals but called hard coral variety and abundance "world class." A 2025 report noted a juvenile humphead wrasse sighting as "maybe a sign of a rebound."
- **Overall: Palau's premier sites are current-swept walls and plateaus where pelagic action, not coral cover, defines the experience. It remains a top-tier destination.**

## Key Dive Sites

#### Blue Corner

5-30m. Palau's signature site and consistently ranked among the world's best dives. A plateau jutting into open ocean where powerful currents aggregate extraordinary marine life. Divers hook into the reef edge at ~15m and watch the show: 20-50+ grey reef sharks on a typical dive, schools of barracuda and bigeye trevally, Napoleon wrasse, eagle rays, and occasional hawksbill turtles. The "reef hook" technique was essentially invented here. Current can exceed 3 knots. Intermediate to advanced.

#### Blue Hole

1-30m. Four openings in the reef top (at ~1m) drop into a cathedral-like cavern, descending to ~25m where a large horizontal window (~25m wide, ~15m high) opens onto the Blue Corner wall. Shafts of light through the ceiling holes create dramatic illumination. Connects to Blue Corner wall for a combination dive. All levels (cavern portion is open and well-lit, not a true cave).

#### German Channel

5-20m. Man-made channel cut through the reef by German phosphate miners in the early 1900s. Now Palau's premier manta cleaning station. Divers kneel on the sandy bottom at ~15m and wait as reef mantas circle overhead at cleaning stations. Manta reliability ~60% of dives, peaking January-April when plankton concentrations are highest. Also grey reef sharks, stingrays, garden eels on the sand flat. All levels; mild to moderate current.

#### Ulong Channel

3-25m. Classic drift dive 15 miles from Koror. Incoming tide carries divers through a channel lined with pristine hard coral gardens over a sandy bottom (10-12m). Grey reef sharks and whitetips patrol the channel mouth; snappers, triggerfish, and groupers aggregate for spawning (peak April-July). Strong, sometimes unpredictable currents. Advanced; reef hook and SMB required.

#### Big Drop-Off (Ngemelis Wall)

1-60m+. A sheer wall plunging from near-surface to 285m+, running the length of Ngemelis Island. Considered one of the world's finest wall dives. Covered in gorgonian fans, purple soft corals, and leather corals. Napoleon wrasse, anthias, butterflyfish, lionfish, nudibranchs in recesses. Grey reef sharks and eagle rays at the corner. Intermediate.

#### Peleliu Wall and Peleliu Corner

10-40m+. Advanced-only sites at Palau's southern tip where Philippine Sea and Pacific Ocean currents converge — the strongest currents in Palau. The wall plateau descends from 10m to 30m, then drops to a deeper plateau at ~40m (Peleliu Corner). Big animal territory: bull sharks, occasional tiger sharks, hammerheads, silky sharks, and rare sightings of whale sharks, pygmy orcas, and even sperm whales reported. Down-currents are common and dangerous. AOW with 100+ dives recommended. SMB mandatory.

#### Peleliu Cut (Peleliu Express)

10-30m. Drift dive along Peleliu's western reef edge, feeding into Peleliu Corner. Fast-moving current delivers an adrenaline ride past sharks and large pelagics. Advanced.

#### Siaes Corner

5-30m. Outer reef wall on Palau's western barrier reef. On the right tide, aggregations of 100-300+ grey reef sharks — rivaling or exceeding Blue Corner density. Strong currents. Advanced.

#### Chandelier Cave

3-12m. Five interconnected cavern chambers accessible from a shallow reef near Koror. Stalactites and stalagmites resembling chandeliers; air pockets allow surfacing inside chambers. Mandarin fish in rubble areas. All levels (guided).

#### WWII Wrecks

30+ Japanese WWII wrecks from Operation Desecrate One (March 1944) and Operation Forager (June 1944). **Iro Maru** (140m fleet oiler, upright and intact, 25-37m, 10 min from Koror — Palau's most popular wreck); **Amatsu Maru** (159m oil tanker, largest wreck in Micronesia, 25-40m, encrusted in black coral); **Jake Seaplane** (15m depth, beginner-friendly, photogenic). 60+ vessels sunk across the lagoon.

## Marine Life

- **Sharks**: Grey reef sharks are the defining species — Blue Corner delivers 20-50 per dive reliably, Siaes Corner up to 300+ on good days. Whitetip reef sharks common across most sites. Bull sharks at Peleliu (not guaranteed but regular). Occasional hammerheads, tiger sharks, and silky sharks at southern sites. Shark populations considered stable or increasing under the 2009 sanctuary.
- **Manta rays**: Reef mantas resident year-round at German Channel cleaning station. Peak reliability January-April. Population individually identified and monitored.
- **Napoleon wrasse**: Protected since 2006 (total ban on extraction, possession, sale); commonly seen at Blue Corner, Big Drop-Off, and wall sites. Juveniles reported in recent trip reports — positive population signal.
- **Bumphead parrotfish**: Protected since 2006. Schools aggregate at specific sites during new moon for spawning — one documented site hosts the largest bumphead aggregation on the planet.
- **Turtles**: Green and hawksbill turtles widespread, commonly seen at Blue Corner and wall sites.
- **Other pelagics**: Eagle rays, schools of barracuda, bigeye trevally, giant trevally, snappers, spawning groupers (Ulong Channel, April-July). Dolphins in open water transits.
- **Macro**: Mandarin fish (Chandelier Cave area), nudibranchs, leaf scorpionfish, lionfish in wall recesses.
- **Saltwater crocodiles**: Native population present, primarily in mangrove estuaries and river systems. Encounters while reef diving are extremely unlikely. No fatal attack since the 1960s.

## Dive Conditions

- **Visibility**: 15-40m+ depending on season and site. Best January-April, often exceeding 40m at outer reef sites. Can drop to 15-20m July-September.
- **Temperature**: 28-30C year-round. 3mm wetsuit sufficient for most; 5mm for repetitive/deep diving.
- **Currents**: The defining characteristic of Palau diving. Blue Corner, Ulong Channel, and especially Peleliu regularly see 2-3+ knot currents. Reef hooks are standard equipment (not optional at Blue Corner). Down-currents at Peleliu can be dangerous. Drift diving skills and SMB deployment are essential.
- **Experience level**: Blue Corner, German Channel, Big Drop-Off, and Chandelier Cave accessible to AOW divers comfortable with current. Ulong Channel and Siaes Corner require drift diving experience. Peleliu sites are advanced-only (AOW + 100 dives minimum).

## Season

- **Dry season (October-May)**: Best overall conditions. Calm seas, light winds, peak visibility.
- **Best months**: January-April — flattest seas, 40m+ visibility, manta peak season at German Channel, bumphead spawning aggregations around new moons.
- **Wet season (June-September)**: More rainfall, reduced visibility (15-20m), stronger winds, rougher seas. Still diveable but outer sites (Peleliu) may be inaccessible on rough days.
- **Typhoon risk**: Palau sits south of the main typhoon belt; direct hits are rare. Occasional tropical disturbances June-November can disrupt schedules.

## Practical Info

- **Access**: Fly to Roman Tmetuchl International Airport (ROR) on Babeldaob, connected to Koror by bridge. United Airlines via Guam (primary US/Asia routing). Korean Air from Seoul. China Airlines/Starlux from Taipei. Qantas launched weekly nonstop Brisbane-Palau service in December 2024.
- **Liveaboard operators**: Palau Siren (luxury phinisi), Palau Aggressor II, Rock Islands Aggressor, Ocean Hunter III. Typical trip: 7 nights, 3-4 dives/day plus night dives.
- **Day-boat operators**: Sam's Tours, Fish 'n Fins, Palau Dive Adventures, Splash, Blue Marlin — all Koror-based. All major sites (Blue Corner, German Channel, Ulong Channel) are reachable by day boat from Koror (45-90 min ride), typically running 2-3 dives/day and returning mid-afternoon. Liveaboards offer more dives per day (up to 5), night dives, and easier Peleliu access.
- **Permits and fees**: **PPEF (Pristine Paradise Environmental Fee)**: $100 per international visitor, collected on arrival. **Rock Islands Permit**: $50/person, valid 5 days (two permits needed for a 7-night trip). **Jellyfish Lake Permit**: ~$100 additional. **Peleliu Permit**: $60 for up to 5 days. **Ngarchelong (northern sites)**: $60 for up to 4 days. Permits subject to change; verify before travel.
- **Recompression chamber**: Located at Belau National Hospital in Koror. Having an on-island chamber is a significant safety advantage over many remote liveaboard destinations.

### Jellyfish Lake

- **Location**: Ongeim'l Tketau, a marine lake on Mecherchar Island in the Rock Islands.
- **What happened**: Golden jellyfish (Mastigias papua etpisoni) population collapsed from millions to fewer than 5,600 — linked to elevated water temperatures disrupting the jellyfish's symbiotic zooxanthellae (same mechanism as coral bleaching). Lake closed to visitors during the worst decline.
- **Historical pattern**: Similar collapses occurred during the 1998 and 2016 El Ninos. Both times, the population rebounded within 2-3 years once conditions normalized.
- **2026 status**: Recovery underway. The Palau Aggressor II reintroduced Jellyfish Lake to its itinerary in early 2026, reporting jellyfish numbers "thriving again." Moon jellyfish remained present in healthy numbers throughout the decline. Lake is open (separate $100 permit; snorkeling only, no scuba).
- **Comparable experience**: [Kakaban Island](indonesia/derawan-kakaban.md) in the Derawan Archipelago (East Kalimantan, Indonesia) has a similar stingless jellyfish lake — one of only a few in the world. As of 2024, Kakaban's population remains healthy with no Palau-style crash reported. See the Derawan section under Indonesia.

## Conservation

- **Palau National Marine Sanctuary (PNMS)**: Enacted 2015, fully in effect since January 1, 2020. Protects 80% of Palau's EEZ (~500,000 km2) as a fully no-take marine reserve — one of the largest MPAs in the world. Remaining 20% is a domestic fishing zone for local use only.
- **Shark Sanctuary**: Established 2009 — Palau was the first nation globally to declare its entire EEZ a shark sanctuary, banning all commercial shark fishing. Credited with maintaining Palau's exceptional shark populations.
- **Species protection**: Bumphead parrotfish and Napoleon wrasse extraction, possession, and sale banned since 2006. Seven of nine global giant clam species found in Palau.
- **Funding model**: The PPEF ($100/visitor) directly funds sanctuary surveillance, enforcement, and administration. The permit system generates additional conservation revenue. Palau has effectively made tourism pay for conservation — a model studied internationally.

## Trip Reports

- **2024:** "Schools of barracuda, bigeye trevally are always there. Humphead wrasse and sharks are also very common." Noted some bleached table coral but overall hard coral variety "world class."
- **Apr 2025:** "One big plus about Palau is the lack of overcrowding at the dive sites — most of the time, we had the sites to ourselves." Good shark action at Blue Corner and Blue Hole.
- **General consensus**: Palau's pelagic-driven sites remain fully intact. Coral shows some bleaching stress but the signature experience — sharks, walls, currents — is undiminished. Jellyfish Lake recovering. Uncrowded relative to comparable destinations.
- **2017–2024 reports:** Nov 2024: vibrant coral gardens, soft coral-covered wrecks (Iro Maru), no bleaching; manta activity strongest Nov–Feb. Mass spawning aggregations documented at Peleliu: ~1.2 million Sailfin Snapper each March–April; monthly Humphead Parrotfish and Red Snapper spawning cycles at reef edges.
