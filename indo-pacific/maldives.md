# Maldives

## Status

- 2024 was the most severe bleaching on record. Central atolls lost >40% hard coral cover (worst site: 57% loss in Ari Atoll). Branching Acropora mortality up to 93-99% at some stations.
- **Southern atolls (Huvadhoo) show significantly greater resilience** — all major coral growth forms still well-represented in 2025.
- Historical recovery time: 16 years between events, but events now occurring every 4-8 years.
- **Recommendation: strongly prefer southern atoll itineraries.** Pelagic life (sharks, mantas at Hanifaru Bay, whale sharks) remains excellent everywhere.
- **Depth-tiered recovery:** Reefs deeper than 5–6m are recovering well while very shallow areas remain dominated by algae-covered rubble. Plan dive profiles accordingly.
- **Resort-level variation is wide:** Filitheyo (North Ari Atoll) described as "excellent — no better house reef" as of March 2025 — a genuine outlier. Bathala house reef was described as "dead." Kandolhu (North Ari) had ~80% house reef in good condition but some sections transformed to "coral graveyards." In the south, Addu Atoll (500km from Male) continues as the best-preserved atoll. Vilamendhoo (South Male) rated "OK."
- **Positive autumn 2025 signal:** One resort reported its house reef as "healthy, colourful, species-rich" in late 2025 — cautiously encouraging but based on a single account.

## Key Dive Sites

### North and Central Atolls

Coral degraded, pelagic life still outstanding.

#### Hanifaru Bay, Baa Atoll

UNESCO Biosphere Reserve: shallow bay (8-15m), sandy bottom funneling plankton into a natural trap. During SW monsoon (Jun-Nov, peak Aug-Oct), 100-200+ reef mantas aggregate in feeding frenzies — the largest known manta feeding aggregation on Earth. On peak days, dozens of mantas chain-feed simultaneously alongside whale sharks. Snorkeling-only since 2014 (scuba banned to reduce disturbance). Entry is regulated: 100 visitors/day, mandatory guide. Reliability in season is high — operators report encounters on ~80% of visits during peak months. The experience is not dependent on coral condition.

#### Fish Head / Mushimasmingili Thila, North Ari Atoll

Submerged pinnacle (thila) topping at 10m, dropping to 30m+. One of the Maldives' most famous shark dives — resident grey reef sharks, whitetip reef sharks, schools of bluefin trevally, Napoleon wrasse. Strong currents typical; divers hook in on the lee side and watch the show. Coral on the thila itself hit hard in 2024 but the shark aggregation is unaffected.

#### Maaya Thila, South Ari Atoll

Small circular thila, top at 6m, walls to 30m. Renowned as a night dive — whitetip reef sharks hunting, stingrays, moray eels, lionfish. Daytime also excellent for sharks and Napoleon wrasse. Overhangs still harbour soft corals and sea fans.

#### Rasdhoo Madivaru, Rasdhoo Atoll

Open-ocean channel site. The dawn dive for scalloped hammerheads — divers descend to 25-30m on the outer reef slope at first light and wait on the blue. Sightings are seasonal (Dec-Apr) and not guaranteed — perhaps 40-60% encounter rate. Grey reef sharks and eagle rays on most dives regardless.

#### Manta Point (Lankan Reef), North Male Atoll

Cleaning station at 10-15m. Reef mantas visit year-round to be cleaned by wrasses on coral bommies. Accessible, shallow, relatively current-free — one of the easier manta encounters in the Maldives. Multiple mantas on a single dive is common. Coral at the station itself is degraded but mantas continue to use it.

### Southern Atolls

Better coral, fewer boats, longer transits.

#### Huvadhoo Atoll (Gaafu Alifu / Gaafu Dhaalu)

The largest atoll in the Maldives and the standout post-bleaching destination. Channels (kandus) between islands create classic Maldivian current diving — Nilandhoo Kandu, Villingili Kandu, Gemanafushi Kandu. Grey reef sharks, eagle rays, and large Napoleon wrasse patrol the channel mouths. Thilas inside the atoll retain better hard coral cover than anything in the central atolls. Visibility often 25-40m. Few liveaboards venture here, so sites are uncrowded.

#### Fuvahmulah

A standalone island (not an atoll), 30km south of Huvadhoo. This is a distinct destination — the Maldives' premier big-animal site and increasingly recognized as a world-class shark dive. **Tiger sharks** are the headline: 3-4m females patrolling the harbour area at 15-25m depth, encountered year-round with near-daily reliability (claimed >95% success rate by local operators). Also: **thresher sharks** at the deep cleaning station (30-40m, early morning), **oceanic manta rays** (the larger species, Mobula birostris, wingspan to 5m+), **scalloped hammerheads** in blue water, **whale sharks** seasonally, and **mola mola** (ocean sunfish) — an unusual sighting for the tropics. Access is by domestic flight from Male to Fuvahmulah airport, then day-boat operations. No liveaboard access — the island has no safe harbour for large vessels. Advanced diving: open-ocean entries, strong currents, deep profiles.

#### Addu Atoll (Seenu)

The southernmost atoll, straddling the equator. **British Loyalty wreck** — a WWII tanker scuttled in 1946, sitting upright at 16-33m, heavily encrusted with soft corals and sponges, resident nurse sharks. Maa Kandu and Kuda Kandu channels for grey reef sharks and eagle rays. Coral condition better than central atolls. Relatively few visiting liveaboards. Addu has its own international airport (Gan) with direct flights from Colombo.

## Marine Life

- **Manta rays**: the Maldives hosts the world's largest identified population of reef mantas (Mobula alfredi) — over 5,000 individuals photo-ID'd by the Manta Trust. Hanifaru Bay feeding aggregation (Jun-Nov) is the peak spectacle. Cleaning station encounters available year-round at multiple atolls. Oceanic mantas (M. birostris) at Fuvahmulah and occasionally deep south channels.
- **Whale sharks**: South Ari Atoll Marine Protected Area is the most reliable year-round whale shark destination in the Maldives — juvenile sharks (3-8m) feed on surface plankton. Primarily snorkeling encounters from dhonis. Sightings consistent year-round but peak Dec-Apr. The population appears stable despite bleaching.
- **Sharks**: grey reef sharks abundant at channel sites throughout all atolls — the most commonly encountered shark. Whitetip reef sharks on thilas and night dives. Blacktip reef sharks in shallows and lagoons. Nurse sharks resting under overhangs. Tiger sharks reliably at Fuvahmulah. Scalloped hammerheads at Rasdhoo and Fuvahmulah (seasonal, not guaranteed). The Maldives banned shark fishing in 2010 (all species, all waters) — shark populations have measurably recovered since. Occasional sightings of silvertip, oceanic whitetip (rare, deep south), and guitar sharks.
- **Other megafauna**: hawksbill and green turtles common at cleaning stations and on reefs. Spinner dolphins in large pods (100+) encountered on surface intervals and boat crossings. Eagle rays at channel mouths, sometimes in large formations (20+).
- **Reef life**: despite bleaching losses, reef fish diversity remains high — Maldives hosts 1,100+ fish species. Frogfish, leaf scorpionfish, ghost pipefish, and nudibranchs present but the Maldives is not primarily a macro destination. Coral loss has reduced habitat for coral-dependent species (butterflyfish, damselfish) at degraded sites.

## Dive Conditions

- **Visibility**: typically 15-30m, occasionally 40m+ in southern atolls and outer reef walls. Reduced to 10-15m inside channels during plankton blooms (which attract mantas and whale sharks — a trade-off). Visibility generally better Dec-Apr (NE monsoon).
- **Temperature**: 27-30C year-round. Thermoclines on deeper dives can drop to 24-25C. No wetsuit heavier than 3mm needed; many divers use rashguards only.
- **Currents**: this is the defining feature of Maldives diving. Channel (kandu) diving involves riding tidal currents through atoll passes — incoming currents bring clear oceanic water and pelagics, outgoing currents push nutrient-rich lagoon water out (attracting filter feeders). Currents range from gentle drift to 3+ knots in narrow channels. Reef hooks are standard equipment for holding position at channel mouths to watch sharks. Current direction and strength dictate every dive plan.
- **Difficulty**: ranges widely. Cleaning stations and inner-atoll sites are suitable for Open Water divers. Channel dives require Advanced certification and current experience. Fuvahmulah and exposed outer reef sites are advanced to expert — open blue descents, unpredictable currents, deep profiles. Most liveaboards require minimum 30-50 logged dives and Advanced OW.

## Season

- **NE monsoon (Dec-Apr, "dry season")**: best visibility (20-30m+), calmest seas, most reliable diving conditions. Prime hammerhead season at Rasdhoo. Whale sharks peak in South Ari. Mantas shift to eastern atoll sides (following plankton). This is peak tourist season — liveaboards book 6-12 months ahead and cost more.
- **SW monsoon (May-Nov, "wet season")**: plankton blooms on western atoll sides attract mantas — **Hanifaru Bay peaks Aug-Oct**. Whale sharks still present. Visibility lower (10-20m) but marine life often more active. Seas rougher on western exposures; liveaboards adjust routes accordingly. Prices lower, availability better.
- **Transitional months (Apr-May, Nov-Dec)**: often excellent — combining reasonable visibility with good marine life activity. November is particularly well-regarded.
- **Sweet spot**: January-March for all-round conditions and shark encounters. August-October for Hanifaru Bay manta spectacle.

## Practical Info

- **Access**: Velana International Airport (MLE), Male. Major hub with direct flights from Colombo (1h), Dubai (4h), Singapore (4.5h), European cities (10-12h via Gulf carriers). Most liveaboards depart from Male harbour. Southern itineraries may use domestic flights to Kaadedhdhoo (Huvadhoo), Fuvahmulah, or Gan (Addu).
- **Liveaboard routes**: **Central classic** (North/South Male, Ari — 7 nights, the standard route, most operators) — good pelagics but worst-hit coral. **Northern** (Baa Atoll, Raa, Lhaviyani — 7 nights) — Hanifaru Bay focus during manta season. **Deep South** (Huvadhoo, Fuvahmulah area, Addu — 7-10 nights) — best coral, fewest boats, longer open-water crossings. **Best of Maldives** (Male to Addu or reverse — 10-14 nights) — premium itineraries covering the full north-to-south range.
- **Operators**: large fleet — 150+ liveaboards operate in the Maldives, ranging from budget to ultra-luxury. Established names include Four Seasons Explorer (luxury), Carpe Diem fleet (mid-range, multiple boats), MV Scubaspa (spa + diving hybrid), Emperor fleet (reliable mid-range), Blue Force (deep south specialists). All-inclusive with 2-3 dives/day standard.
- **Trip length**: 7 nights standard for single-region routes; 10-14 nights for cross-atoll or deep south expeditions. Short 4-5 night trips exist but limit route options.
- **Recompression chambers**: Bandos Island (North Male Atoll) operates a well-equipped hyperbaric facility staffed by physicians — the primary chamber for the country. A second chamber exists at Kuramathi (Rasdhoo Atoll). Southern atolls are hours from either chamber — deep south itineraries carry elevated DCI risk due to distance from treatment.
- **Experience level**: most operators require Advanced OW + 30-50 logged dives minimum. Nitrox certification strongly recommended (and often included). Deep south and Fuvahmulah require genuine experience — not the place to log dive #31.

## Conservation

- **Baa Atoll UNESCO Biosphere Reserve (2011)**: includes Hanifaru Bay. Entry fees, daily visitor caps (100), mandatory guides, scuba ban in the bay. The management framework is considered a model for the Maldives but enforcement quality varies by season and political will.
- **Shark fishing ban (2010)**: all shark species protected in all Maldivian waters. This was driven by economic analysis showing a live reef shark is worth $33,500/year in tourism vs $32 dead. Enforcement is imperfect — illegal finning still occasionally reported — but shark populations have visibly recovered over the past 15 years. No formal "shark sanctuary" designation exists yet (unlike Palau) but the functional protection is similar.
- **South Ari Marine Protected Area**: designated to protect the whale shark aggregation. Approach regulations exist (3m minimum distance, no flash photography, no touching) but compliance from speedboat operators is inconsistent.
- **Environmental challenges**: the Maldives faces waste management problems — Thilafushi (the "trash island" near Male) burns waste including plastics, visible from nearby dive sites. Coral mining was historically practiced for construction material and was not fully banned until 2009; legacy damage from extraction is still visible on some reefs. Groundwater lens contamination from septic systems affects nearshore reef health at inhabited islands. Sea level rise (average elevation 1.5m) is the existential long-term threat.
- **Coral restoration**: resort-funded coral frame projects are widespread (100+ resorts have nurseries) but most are small-scale and primarily serve guest engagement rather than ecological recovery. Larger-scale efforts include the Maldives Coral Institute (est. 2022) working on heat-resistant coral strains and the Maldives Marine Research Institute's monitoring network. Whether any restoration effort can outpace bleaching frequency is an open question.

## Trip Reports

- Multiple 2024-2025 reports from central atoll routes describe "white graveyards" and "rubble fields" where Acropora gardens once stood. Fish life still abundant but the reef framework is visibly destroyed at shallow sites in North/South Male and Ari. "The fish are still there but swimming over skeletons."
- A January 2025 Ari Atoll liveaboard report noted excellent shark encounters at Fish Head and Maaya Thila but called shallow coral "the worst I have seen in 20 years of diving the Maldives."
- Southern reports are markedly more positive: a March 2025 Huvadhoo trip described "colourful hard coral gardens — brain corals, table corals, staghorns still intact" with "noticeably fewer boats than the central atolls."
- Fuvahmulah reports from late 2024 through early 2026 consistently describe tiger shark encounters as "like clockwork — multiple tigers on every harbour dive." Thresher shark sighting rates vary more (estimated 50-70% success at the cleaning station).
- A recurring theme: divers who visited the Maldives pre-2024 and returned in 2025 describe the central atolls as "unrecognizable" for coral but emphasize that pelagic encounters are as good or better than ever — sharks, mantas, and whale sharks are not coral-dependent.
- **2024–2026 reports:** Confirm the north/south split. General assessment: "house reefs are more or less all in bad condition, at least regarding stone corals." Filitheyo stands out as a positive exception. January 2026 Spirit of Maldives liveaboard: healthy fish aggregations, manta and whale shark encounters. The depth-tiered recovery pattern (deeper = better) independently confirmed across multiple recent dives.
